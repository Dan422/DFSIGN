Squad Harmonix

Web Print Brand

Website, branding, and graphics for a kids choir and training academy

Background

Talented musician and dear friend of mine Laura Saggers started Squad Harmonix with the hope of not only gathering Los Angeles' most talented kids for a voice performance group, but also growing it into an academy.

Opportunity

As Laura acquired staff and talent, Squad Harmonix became more and more known in the city. This kind of growth demanded a modern, fresh brand and web presence so it can scale. A clear auditioning system, consistent content creation, and strong brand reach were key business goals.

Hypothesis

User: I believe more auditions and signings will occur by providing clear, educational steps up front through a mobile-first website.

Business: I believe strong brand recognition and engagement will be achieved by designing a unique, modern, and fun design language, logo, and mark.

Website Design and Development


Skip to Outcome

Kick-Off

Conversations and Questions

We started by outlining user and business outcomes and key apects started to emerge:

1.) Increase audition and camp signups
2.) Design around media presentation
3.) Emphasize performance dates for awareness.

#1 was the most crucial for growing the academy, and since that aligned with both user and business desires, I focused on that.

Form Hypothesis

After compiling notes, I put together a clear hypothesis statement:

I believe more auditions and signings will occur by providing clear, educational steps up front through a mobile-first website.

Empathize

Understand the User

With these goals defined, I wanted to learn more about the user persona we were dealing with. Most of this was inherited through Laura's experiences with working in the Los Angeles music industry:

Demographic

  • Working parent
  • Based in Los Angeles
  • Travels frequently
  • Mobile-first
  • High income

Needs

  • Creative opportunities for their child
  • Instructional training and focus practice
  • Peace of mind

Behaviors

  • Passionate about their child's talents
  • Local and community influenced
  • Looks for new opportunties
  • Gratitude for their child's development

Ideation

Key Aspects

From there, I drew context, content, and user aspects that also align with the business goal of gaining more auditions and brand recognition:

  • Call-to-action: Have "Sign Up" and "Audition" links as prominent and frequent as possible
  • Clear and informative: Display onboarding process for each camp/level clearly and avoid cognitive overload
  • Engaging: Keep them on the page with fun, kid-friendly design elements and language
Ideas
  • "Sign Up" and "Audition" call-to-actions in the navigation and above the fold
  • Breakdown auditioning process into bullets or numbered steps
  • Emphasize video content
  • Create a carousel of participant testimonials
  • Display a calendar of events
Design Goals

I then visualized and noted design-level goals:

  • Familiar visual hierarchy
  • Bullets over paragraphs
  • Exposed navigation
  • Fun and clean design language
  • Bold and modern typography

I used these ideas to start composing information architecture and early sketches of the site layout.




Information architecture is great way to blueprint your site's views, hierarchy, content and navigation.

Sketch, Wireframe, Iterate

Hand Sketches

With information architecture paving the way for the site's navigation and content, I started sketching some of these pages to life. Simple hand drawings make it much easier to iterate and exercise design ideas.

Refine Into Wireframes

Using prototyping tools Whimsical and Adobe XD, I refined some of the stronger sketches into wireframes. This helped us visualize the product easier before applying design language, brand, content, and imagery.

  View Wireframe

Incorporate Feedback and Iterate

A few rounds of feedback and rapid prototyping led to a concrete design that paved way for development.




Version 1 higher fidelity wireframes.

Develop

Approach

With a design settled on, I used framework Bootstrap 4 as a boilerplate to start developing the site. Some key areas I wanted to focus on:

  • Top-level navigation: always exposed and accessible
  • Content panels: engaging imagery coupled with informative bullets and content
  • Hero: Bold title, clear value proposition, and a single call-to-action
Language and Components

I then broke views down into clusters of components that became more complex:

Simple

  • Buttons
  • Forms
  • Typography
  • Color palette
  • Imagery/graphic standards

Complex

  • Navigation and footer bars
  • Hero banner
  • Content panels
  • Data tables
  • Media columns
Putting it All Together

By approaching each views as just groups of components, it made development time much more efficient and consistent. Instead of large, custom CSS classes, everything was broken out and easily accessible on the component level, backed up by the power of helper and utility classes.

The Outcome

After several rounds of testing, the site was launched and immediately started seeing growth in auditions, sign ups, and engagement.

Brand, Graphic, and Print Design


Skip to Outcome

Inspiration

Initial Direction

Laura and her team invisioned a brand that was fun, spunky, and kid-friendly. After bouncing a few ideas around, the "80's theme" became a constant. She loved bright, neon colors like green, pink, blue and purple that would end up being the building blocks of the brand's design language.

Examples and Insight

With this in mind, I put together an inspiration board with examples that conveyed this 80's genre and vibe. Anything from movie posters, album covers, and soda labels, to sports and fashion figures, I drew inspiration from. A few key desires were discussed as well:

  • Resonate with not only kids but their parents as well
  • Strive for "hip", "modern", and "cool"
Form Hypothesis

I then put together a clear hypothesis statement:

I believe strong brand recognition and engagement will be achieved by designing a unique, modern, and fun design language, logo, and mark.

Define Design Language

Theme

A brand's design should convey its meaning, feeling, and purpose. A good way to accomplish this is to list out thematic elements:

  • Fun and spunky, not too serious
  • Modern and unconventional
  • Bright, heavy contrast
Building Blocks

Using themes to drive design language decisions, I broke it down into building blocks:

  • Color palette: neon, complimentary
  • Shapes: Round and flat, asymmetrical
  • Typography: modern, sans-serif, condensed
  • Metaphors: impact and overlay

Define Logo and Mark

Ideas

With general branding direction established, I started on the logo. We wanted something that tied in with the actual "Squad Harmonix" and "X" letters. Ideas began to emerge:

  • Since we perform in various venues, a constant is the microphone; projecting "voice"
  • A mark that can be universally identified within the music community
  • Modern and relatable; bold and in-your-face, "loud"
  • Kids coming together to sing together
Sketches

I took to my grid paper and started drawing elements and objects that represented these ideas, focusing on the logo mark. Laura and I collaborated in this phase and came up with some solid options revolving around the "microphone" and "together" ideas.

Digitize and Refine

We gravitated towards the "X" shape form with the microphone objects and I translated them into Adobe Illustrator for refinement. After a few iterations, I designed the final logo and mark variations.



Final logo and mark variations.

The Outcome

We then brought all the inspiration, themes, and design language elements to life through web, graphic, and print design. From hoodies to flyers, we let the brand grow and shape into something unique and special for our audience.
