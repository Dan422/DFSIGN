Offerpad

UI/UX

Pricing page redesign for a disrupting home buying and selling company

Background

Offerpad approached me to redesign their front-facing pricing page. The goals of the page were 1.) illustrate to the seller how competitive Offerpad is compared to the traditional method of selling a home and 2.) convert them into an Offerpad user for the sale.

Problem

The original page displayed static, industry-default values that only gave users a one-dimensional view of their price comparison. High bounce rates occured because 1.) users were unable to input their own prices to truly see the value Offerpad provides and 2.) were confused by the provided real estate terminology.

Hypothesis

I believe higher user engagement and sales requests will be achieved by allowing prospective home sellers to input and customize their own values in clear, educational steps.

Timeline

1 Month, March 2019

Website Design

Kick-Off

Conversations and Questions

I started by asking the Offerpad team a lot of questions: Where did the need for this came from? What metrics were gathered? What are the user and business outcomes expected? Since this was a short-term consultation, I prioritized time talking with key stakeholders up-front so I can get a clear voice of the user.

Compile Feedback

By gathering every email and phone conversation, I started writing out a lean design thinking brief focusing on the desired user and business outcome. The result was a core set of running documents I could refer to that keep users at the heart of the process.

Form Hypothesis

After compiling notes, I put together a clear hypothesis statement:

I believe higher user engagement and sales requests will be achieved by allowing prospective home sellers to input and customize their own values in clear, educational steps.

  View Final Brief

Empathize and Define

Understand the User

Using this feedback and empathy mapping, I started to list out aspects of the user persona we were working with:

Demographic

  • People actively looking to sell their home
  • 60% were mobile
  • Older audience with an influx of younger
  • Do-it-yourself mindset
  • Newer to the real estate market

Needs

  • See how much their home is worth today
  • Sell their home at a reasonable price
  • How much they can profit or lose from a sale

Behaviors

  • Use multiple online price calculators
  • Search and call agents for consultation
Problems

Then I plotted pain points along the user journey gathered from metrics, support calls, and internal reviews:

  • Page is static and uninviting
  • Main call-to-actions are buried further down the page
  • Not enough differentiation between Offerpad vs. traditional method
  • Users need a way to enter their own values

Ideate Solutions

Key Aspects

With user needs and problems in mind, I drew key aspects that also align with the business goal of gaining more conversions:

  • Make it their offer: Be able to enter and change their own home values
  • Educational: Background and explain real estate terms directly and plainly
  • Clear steps: Ease them into their final offer and create the sale conversion
Ideas
  • Have initial offer entry form with value proposition highest in the page
  • Break the current static table into separate sections/steps
  • Allow for user input and customization, i.e. forms, sliders, steppers, etc.
  • Expose educational snippets or click/hover popovers to show simpler real estate info to the user
  • Show progress indication
Design Goals

I then visualized and noted design-level goals:

  • Familiar visual hierarchy
  • Welcoming tone and content
  • Clear visual separation between Offerpad vs. tradiation method
  • Expose educational snippets or click/hover popovers to show simpler real estate info to the user
  • Educational steps that ease into the final price

By using those key aspects and ideas to drive my initial design sketches and prototypes, I was able to quickly iterate and continually improve the designs.


Sketch, Wireframe, Iterate

Hand Sketches

After much back-and-forth with stakeholders, tweaking ideas, and landing on some concrete solutions, I pulled out my handy grid and printer paper and sketched away. I maybe went through dozens of sheets of concepts before deciding on one to go with. While sketching, I always list out the design goals and key aspects alongside so that they aren't forgotten.

Refine Into Wireframes

Using prototyping tools Whimsical and Adobe XD, I refined some of the stronger sketches into wireframes. This type of design helps stakeholders and users visualize the product easier before applying design language, brand, content, and imagery.
